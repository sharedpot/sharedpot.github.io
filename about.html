Why communal cooking?

A cooking circle is a small group of neighbours who take turns cooking large batches for each other. One person cooks once a week or once a month; the rest of the time they're fed by someone else. It is one of the oldest forms of mutual aid, scaled down to a kitchen.

The math is straightforward. Buying a bag of rice, a sack of onions, or a whole chicken is dramatically cheaper per portion than buying for one. A cook who makes eight portions of stew uses roughly the same time and energy as a cook who makes two — the chopping is the same, the pot is the same, the cleanup is the same. Multiply that by everyone in the circle and you get hours back every week and a noticeably smaller grocery bill.

Beyond money and time, the social part is the point. You eat food made by someone who knows you. You learn dishes you would never have cooked yourself. You build the kind of low-stakes, regular contact with neighbours that turns a building or a block into a community. When something goes wrong — a job loss, an illness, a new baby — there is already a network in place that knows how to show up with a meal.

It's also less wasteful: bulk cooking means less packaging, fewer half-rotten vegetables in the back of one fridge, and meals that get eaten instead of forgotten.

How to start a circle

Start small. Three or four people is plenty. More than six is hard to coordinate without a system.

  • Pick a rhythm. Weekly is common. Each person cooks once per cycle. If you have four people, you cook once a month and eat from the circle three other weeks.
  • Pick a channel. A Telegram, WhatsApp, or Signal group works. Use it to confirm the week's plan, share dietary needs, and coordinate handoffs.
  • Agree on portions and containers. Decide upfront: are we feeding individuals or whole households? Does everyone bring their own jars? A standard set of reused containers (yoghurt tubs, mason jars) saves arguments.
  • Talk about diet on day one. Allergies, vegetarian or vegan preferences, things people genuinely won't eat. Write it down somewhere everyone can see.
  • Decide how money works. Most circles don't bother splitting costs — over a full rotation, ingredient costs even out roughly. If your costs are wildly uneven, agree on a flat per-portion contribution or a shared kitty.
  • Cook safely. Cool food fast, refrigerate within two hours, label with the date, and don't ferment things you don't understand. If your kitchen isn't up to feeding several households, fix that before you scale up.
  • Plan for drops. Someone will get sick or travel. Decide in advance: do you skip their week, do they cook double next time, do you order pizza? Just have an answer.

How to participate

Find a circle on the map near you. Click through to the chat link and introduce yourself: who you are, where you live, whether you have dietary restrictions, and what you can cook.

For your first time as a guest, ask what to bring (often: a clean container and your own appetite). Be on time for handoffs. If you're picking food up, do it within the agreed window — leftovers don't keep forever.

When it's your turn to cook, cook food you actually like and can make well. The circle isn't a cooking show; it's dinner. Make enough that nobody has to ration. Label everything with date and ingredients.

Don't see a circle near you? Add one. Most circles start with two friends and a shared spreadsheet — yours can too.
